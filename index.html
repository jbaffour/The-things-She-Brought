As a result, Helena needed to have it transported from Ghana to the U.S. Because shipping internationally wasn't yet common in Ghana, it often had to be given to anyone who was traveling to the U.S. This also served as a perfect vehicle for her family back home to send her money. Helena is coming to America with the knowledge and business of the baking industry. In Ghana, she was a successful flour and sugar importer. Her store in Assin Fosu, supplied most of the Central Region of Ghana with Sugar and Flour. Her hope is to continue her business in the United States.

After getting fired from her restaurant job because she was pregnant, she started her own business selling Pastries to other West African immigrants at the Bronx Terminal Market. The business was really sucessful, seeing her pull in about $300 to $400 everyweek, which was more than what she was making at the Restaurant. Unfortunately, the business got shut down when the city decided to shut down the market to build a mall, dispersing the group of small but diverse group of immigrant entreprenuers

Unbeknowest to Helena, she is also pregnant with her third child Edward. The pregnancy and subsequent child care will place a wrench in her career plans.

After Helena’s Bofrot business got shut down as a result of the City’s planning, she wanted to pursue formal education to open more job opportunities however, as the sole breadwinner of her family, she needed a quick stable income. There was no time to pursue further education in the U.S, so she settled to become a Home Health Aide which had a low barrier of entry.

This is Helena Baffour, a Ghanaian mother who is about to leave her country in search of a better future for her family. Similar to other Ghanaians during this time, she wants to leave the country to better the life of her family by positioning herself in the center of economic production, which basically means the West but in her case the United States. In 2003, the year she embarked on her journey, Ghana was priming itself to become an economic powerhouse in West Africa, however, the avenues that paved that were supposed to pave the way for this was restricted to a select few. So Helena and several thousand others left everything they knew and loved behind. They carried some things with them, these are Helena’s.

19 years after Helena left Ghana, her eldest son, James graduated from NYU, making one of her dreams come true.
